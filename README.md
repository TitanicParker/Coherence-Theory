Coherence Theory — A Familiar Explanation of What We’ve Discovered
Imagine someone asked you to explain how a sentence makes sense — not what it means, but how meaning itself holds together. For thousands of years we’ve taken this for granted. We speak, we listen, we read, we feel when a sentence “lands” or “comes together,” and we move on. No one ever questions how we knew the sentence was finished, or how we understood the relationships between its pieces. It’s as natural as breathing.
Coherence Theory — or what we now call the Study of Sentences — says something simple but extremely powerful:

Every coherent sentence follows the same underlying geometric process.
Meaning isn’t invented by language; it emerges from a universal structure the mind already runs.

This isn’t an academic theory. It’s a description of something everyone already does without noticing. Kids feel when a sentence is over long before they understand grammar. Adults sense contradiction or incompleteness instantly. This project explains why.
At the heart of coherence theory is a shockingly small idea:

All coherent thought, language, perception, and understanding move through just six operations.
(distinction, expansion, relation, correction, integration, rest)
— described in the White Paper as the operator arc ∇ → div → curl → curl★ → ∇· → I .

These aren’t linguistic categories. They’re not psychological labels. They’re the six moves any mind, any system, and any coherent piece of language must make to hold itself together.
You make a distinction.
You explore its possibilities.
You relate the parts.
You correct mismatch.
You draw the pieces together.
You land in a stable understanding.
Whether you’re solving a problem, telling a story, writing an email, or understanding a child who says “I’m tired,” your mind completes this loop. This is why the Coherence Suitcase says:

“The 60° manifold is the minimal circuit of coherence.” 

It’s the simplest geometric space that allows meaning to stabilize.

Where the 54 Forms Come In
If the six operators are the verbs of coherence, the fifty-four invariants are the nouns — the stable shapes meaning can land in. The White Paper describes these as the required fixed relational forms — the only ones coherence ever stabilizes into across all domains: mathematics, language, physics, reasoning, perception .
This sounds abstract until you realize:
You feel these invariants already.
You feel symmetry in an argument.
You feel equivalence when someone restates a point.

You feel closure when a thought resolves.
Every time you understand anything, your mind is landing on one of those 54 forms. The invariants are not technical constructs — they are the natural stations where coherence comes to rest, the destinations of the sentence-walk every mind performs.
The Learning Log formalizes exactly this: any sentence can be mapped onto an operator arc, submode activity, and invariant landing . What we’ve done is simply “slow down” the process so we can see it.

# Coherence Theory

Coherence Theory proposes that understanding follows a small and repeatable
internal structure, and that ordinary sentences are the simplest place where
this structure becomes visible. The project aims to describe that structure
geometrically and to articulate how coherence moves, stabilises, and completes
itself.

This repository contains the early, formal articulation of that work.

---

## What this project is

Coherence Theory is an attempt to describe coherence itself:  
how meaning begins, how it develops, how it refines, and how it comes to rest.

It is:

- a **geometric account** of the motions of understanding,  
- a study of **operators** (primitive structural actions),  
- an articulation of **invariants** (stable landing forms of meaning),  
- and a method for reading sentences as **trajectories** across this structure.

The project is deliberately conservative.  
The goal is clarity, correctness, and internal coherence rather than scope.

Everything here should be treated as early but stable conceptual groundwork.

## Core structure

For readers who want a deeper view of the framework, the central documents
are located in `docs/`:

- **`docs/core-theory.md`** — the main conceptual statement of Coherence Theory:
  operators, invariants, the 6×3×3 architecture, and why sentences reveal the
  underlying geometry.

- **`docs/operators.md`** — definitions of the six operators and how they operate
  structurally, cognitively, and expressively.

- **`docs/invariants.md`** — an architectural overview of the 54 invariants:
  six operator families, three submodes each, three invariants per submode
  (6×3×3).

- **`docs/geometry.md`** — a description of the hexagonal coherence manifold:
  why six directions, why 60°, and how stable invariant landings emerge.

- **`docs/walking-sentences.md`** — a practical method for tracing a sentence
  through the operator arc to observe coherence unfolding in real time.

These documents form the spine of the project and will be expanded as the work
continues to stabilise.
